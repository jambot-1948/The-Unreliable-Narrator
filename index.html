The Unreliable Narrator

In literature, an unreliable narrator is a storyteller whose account you can't fully trust — not because they're lying, but because their perspective is limited, their memory is selective, or they don't know what they don't know.

The artifacts we rely on at work — dashboards, pipelines, roadmaps, burn-down charts — are narrators too. They tell coherent stories from real data. But each one sees only its own frame. None of them are wrong. None of them are complete.

From far enough away,
every system looks coherent.

We measure everything. We track progress. We are in control.

Rashomon (1950)

In Kurosawa's film, four witnesses describe the same crime. Each account is detailed and emotionally convincing. None agree. The brilliance isn't that someone is lying — it's that each person is telling the truth as they experienced it.

The Same Meeting,

All true. All incompatible.

The Usual Suspects (1995)

A suspect tells a story in an interrogation room. It's coherent, confident, and completely assembled — names, places, and details pulled from objects around him. You trusted the structure of the explanation. That was the trick.
